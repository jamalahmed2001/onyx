# show-dont-say

**Rule.** Demonstrate the point through behaviour, action, or specific detail. Don't state the point as exposition.

**Why.** Stated points slide off — the audience hears words and doesn't picture anything. Demonstrated points stick because the audience builds the inference themselves. "He was nervous" is a label; "his hand kept folding the corner of the paper" is the same fact made visible, and the audience does the inference work that makes it land. The same rule holds for moral points, emotional beats, and even technical claims: a worked example beats a rule statement, every time.

**How to apply.**
- Replace adjective-laden description ("she was patient", "he was furious") with a single specific behaviour ("she waited until the third question to answer", "he set the cup down without drinking").
- For arguments, lead with the example; only state the conclusion when the example demands it.
- For visual content, the camera *shows* what the script would otherwise *say* — eye-line, posture, hands, what's in frame.
- A scene with three demonstrated specifics outperforms a scene with one specific and three labels.
- Exception: in dense expository moments (a complicated mechanism, a list of safety steps), stated points are fine — labels for what's about to happen save the audience from getting lost.

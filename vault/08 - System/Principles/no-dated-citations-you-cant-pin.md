# no-dated-citations-you-cant-pin

**Rule.** A year-stamped citation (`(Source: Publisher — Title, 2025)`) only appears when the brief carries that exact dated publication. Otherwise drop the year and soften the figure.

**Why.** Year-stamped citations imply the writer pinned a specific publication. When the year is a guess, the citation is fabrication that *sounds* responsible. The reader trusts year-stamped attributions more than vague ones — exploiting that trust with an unverified year is the worst kind of credibility-borrowing. The honest move is "(Source: Publisher)" with a softened figure, leaving the reader to look it up if they care.

**How to apply.**
- The research / brief phase carries the actual publication and year for each cited stat.
- The script writer reproduces the citation verbatim from the brief, year and all, *only* when the brief has the year.
- When the brief has the publisher and a rough figure but not a confirmed year, write "(Source: Publisher)" and soften the number ("around a million") rather than "(Source: Publisher, 2025)" with a precise number.
- "Around a million" with a real publisher beats "exactly 1,043,200" with a guessed year. The first is honest; the second sounds confident and is fragile.
- Dated stats that turn out to be wrong are unrecoverable in published content. Soften when uncertain.
